# 2. Checklist before contract

- [ ] Review contract conditions ([Reference](/1d9d96a400a3805e89dfe80baec86843#1d9d96a400a3809d8381fd73da44eb8d))
- [ ] Review deliverables
- [ ] Create a channel for sharing contract-related matters

# 3. Checklist after contract

- [ ] Check the contract amount (advance payment) and address, then execute the advance payment
- [ ] Have you prepared the audit progress document?
- [ ] Have you registered the dates specified in the contract in the Tokamak calendar? Record the audit start date, audit end date, date of receiving the first audit report, deadline for feedback according to the first audit report, advance payment date, balance payment date, etc. in the calendar.
- [ ] Create company-specific communication channels

# 4. Checklist when getting the first report

- [ ] If the first report submission date has passed according to the contract and the first report has not been submitted, contact the auditor and request the report submission.
- [ ] Check if there is an interim payment according to the contract terms.
- [ ] When the first report comes out, notify and share it in the channel.

# 5. Checklist during reflection feedback

- [ ] Check the feedback submission schedule for the first report.
- [ ] Share the feedback report by notifying the channel.
- [ ] Check and share the process and schedule after feedback submission by company in the channel.

# 6. Checklist before final report

- [ ] Check final deliverables
- [ ] Check external disclosure
- [ ] Check contract amount (balance) and address

## Contract review contents

### Payment methods

- Virtual assets preferred (USDT, USDC, etc.)
- Dollar payment is possible (may differ according to the foundation's situation, so foundation confirmation is needed)

### Payment schedule

- Key point: **The final payment should be made after receiving the final report, and if not, it is advisable not to proceed with the contract.**
- Split payments are recommended as follows:
  - 50% at initial start, 50% remaining balance after receiving the final report
  - 30% at initial start, 30% after receiving the first report, 40% after receiving the final report

### Check for late fees and penalties

- When there are late fees and penalties, all foundation representatives and development-related managers must share the content and amount, and record it in the work document.
- If there are penalties, it is desirable to specify them in detail. If there are unfavorable contents, it is better not to enter into the contract.
  - Check for additional fees if there are many vulnerabilities in the audit
    - Check whether vulnerabilities include gas efficiency and code improvement level, or only critical severities such as fund freezing or fund loss
  - (Example) This was content that existed in Omniscia's contract:

I believe the contract with Omniscia for TON staking V2 was misjudged. Apart from the audit progress content, the client responsibility items or the contents regarding out-of-scope work in the audit contract were directly related to development, but we proceeded with the contract without checking these, so we made a contract without knowing the contract contents. This time, we conducted audits with 4 companies simultaneously, and one developer was in charge of the contract with one or two companies, dividing them up. While the contract context can be divided, I learned that **the contract must be reviewed by all developers after receiving it**. Therefore, I thought that all of the above audit contract review contents should be checked to prevent such mistakes from happening again.
Omniscia's contract not only had a different audit scope from what was proposed, but also included the statement that "**Remediation or Consultation: Remediation or consulting services for any security vulnerabilities discovered during the audit are not encompassed within this SOW.**" Paying for an audit but having no consultation on vulnerabilities is not a clause that allows for a comfortable contract.

Omniscia ran an audit program and reported everything from very basic grammar to trivial matters. As a result, there were quite a lot of audit contents suggesting grammatically recommended coding. When all of these are classified as vulnerabilities, the number of reported vulnerabilities increases. Then note that **there is a contract clause that says you have to pay an extra amount if the number of discovered items exceeds a certain number**. This was quite a surprising part. It's difficult to consider it a vulnerability when development is done in a style different from the recommended grammar, and it was hard to accept that additional amounts would be generated because of this.

However, excluding the amount, some of the reported items were based on very accumulated knowledge, so they were (good for study) materials that were helpful to know. However, I will not make the judgment to entrust an audit to Omniscia next time.
